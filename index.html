Curator Cards

Exploring the history and principles of influential design movements.

Bauhaus (1919–1933)
functionalism geometric forms modularity minimalism universal design
The Bauhaus was a design school founded by Walter Gropius in 1919. It sought to unify art, craft, and technology, emphasizing functionalism and rejecting excessive ornamentation. Designers embraced geometric forms and modularity to create objects suitable for mass production. Its focus on universal design shaped modernist principles globally.
→ Bauhaus Collection at MoMA → Bauhaus Archive Museum
Not to be confused with: Swiss Style, which focuses specifically on grid systems and typographic clarity.
Verified via: MoMA and Bauhaus Archive
Swiss Style (1950s–1970s)
grid system sans-serif typography functionalism universal design minimalism
Also known as the International Typographic Style, this movement emerged to address the need for clear communication. It is defined by the grid system and sans-serif typography for maximum readability. By eliminating unnecessary elements, it prioritized minimalism and functionalism, influencing corporate branding worldwide.
→ Josef Müller-Brockmann at MoMA → Swiss Style at Cooper Hewitt
Not to be confused with: Bauhaus, which has a broader focus on industrial craft rather than just typographic systems.
Verified via: Cooper Hewitt Collections
New Wave (1970s–1980s)
grid system dynamic layouts deconstruction expressivity negative space
New Wave Typography reacted against the rigid conventions of Swiss Style. It rejected strict grid systems in favor of dynamic layouts and deconstruction. Utilizing expressive typography and photomontage, the movement reflected a shift toward individualism and postmodern ideals.
→ Wolfgang Weingart at MoMA → New Wave at Cooper Hewitt
Not to be confused with: Grunge, because New Wave retains technical precision while Grunge embraces distressed aesthetics.
Verified via: MoMA Artist Archives
